3D genome organization, chromatin architecture, and computational genomics.

I study how the three-dimensional organization of chromatin shapes gene regulation, evolves across species, and breaks in disease. My work combines genomics, epigenetics, bioinformatics, and machine learning to understand genome architecture in normal development and under chromosomal rearrangements.

Research areas

3D genome organization

Topologically associated domains, chromatin loops, compartments, and the mechanisms that shape nuclear architecture in different cell types and organisms.

Chromosomal rearrangements and disease

Functional interpretation of structural variants and genome rearrangements through the lens of chromatin contacts and epigenetic regulation.

Comparative and evolutionary genomics

How genome architecture changes across species, and what remains conserved over evolution.

Computational genomics and machine learning

Predictive models for enhancer–promoter interactions, chromatin contacts, and context-aware interpretation of noncoding genomic variation.

Software and tools

3DPredictor

A machine-learning framework for quantitative prediction of chromatin interactions, including enhancer–promoter contacts, from limited epigenetic input such as CTCF binding and RNA-seq.

C-InterSecture

A computational pipeline for interspecies comparison of genome architecture and quantitative analysis of conserved or divergent chromatin contacts.

3DGenBench

A benchmark web server for evaluating computational models in 3D genomics and comparing their performance in a transparent way.

Variant interpretation pipelines

Methods and workflows for detecting chromosomal rearrangements and interpreting their regulatory consequences in human disease.

Vision

My long-term goal is to make genome architecture predictive. I am interested in building a bridge from descriptive 3D genomics to practical models that can explain developmental programs, evolutionary change, and the functional consequences of noncoding and structural variants.

This means combining mechanistic insight with computational tools that are robust, interpretable, and useful beyond a small set of benchmark cell types. I believe the next step for the field is not only better measurement of chromatin contacts, but principled prediction: models that can generalize to unseen cell states, new species, and clinically relevant genomic alterations.

In parallel, I care about turning advanced genome-architecture methods into tools that other researchers and clinicians can actually use — especially for disease variant interpretation, comparative genomics, and large-scale functional annotation of the noncoding genome.
